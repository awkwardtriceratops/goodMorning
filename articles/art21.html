‘Please Like Me,’ Trump Begged. For Many Women, It’s Way Too Late.

The white suburban voters the president needs to carve a path to victory have turned away from him, for deeply personal reasons. By Lisa Lerer WESTERVILLE, Ohio — Kate Rabinovitch doesn’t call herself an activist. A few weeks ago, the 29-year-old real estate agent wrote personalized messages to voters in her home state of Ohio on behalf of Joseph R. Biden Jr. She texts with undecided friends during the debates, arguing for the Democratic nominee. And she is helping to organize voter drives in her suburban Cleveland neighborhood. But a political activist? No way. “It’s just not something that I ever would have described myself as, if you talked to me a year ago,” said Ms. Rabinovitch. “I’m just a mom with the feels, like hard feels.” Four years ago, Ms. Rabinovitch agonized over which candidate to support. In the final minutes of voting, she walked into the booth still uncertain. She left having cast her ballot for Donald J. Trump. “I thought, ‘Oh, what’s the worst that could happen?’” she recalled recently. “I do feel guilty.”
