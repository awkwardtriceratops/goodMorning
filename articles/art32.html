Moderation Returns to Politics. Or at Least to the Debate.

CRITIC’S NOTEBOOK Donald Trump and Joe Biden faced off one last time, and the winner was Kristen Welker. By James Poniewozik On Thursday night, Belmont University in Nashville hosted the second and only presidential debate of the 2020 general election. You may remember the jackhammer concert masquerading as a debate three weeks ago, in which President Trump showed up furiously spewing invective (and possibly coronavirus). Your ears are probably still ringing from it. The next scheduled debate was canceled after the president came down with Covid-19 and refused to participate in a virtual face-off, replaced by a ratings war of competing town halls. On Thursday, the president and his opponent, former Vice President Joseph R. Biden Jr., met in a debate with a similar format to the fracas in September — similar subject matter and equally high stakes. But this time there was a mute switch, a moderator who actually committed moderation and multiple complete uninterrupted sentences. It’s a sad statement that the measure of an acceptable debate is that it doesn’t sound like listening to simultaneous auctions. But it’s 2020. We take what we can get. You could attribute the intelligibility of the debate partly to technology. After the September fiasco, the commission on presidential debates made the decision to mute each candidate’s microphone during the other’s two-minute responses. This didn’t prevent cross talk afterward, but it may have provided a cooling-off period. But much credit belongs to the moderator, Kristen Welker of NBC News, who kept a confident hand and a quick mind trained on the discussion beginning to end. Keeping order in a debate isn’t just a matter of interjecting and pleading. Just ask Chris Wallace, who ended his outing in September flattened like a Looney Tunes character who lost a fight with an anvil. (“I’m jealous,” he said on Fox after the Thursday debate.) Instead, Ms. Welker was firm and agile, finding the moments to quickly follow up and heading off filibusters — not every one, but many — with a crisp, “OK; let’s move on.” For all the talk of “law and order” in this campaign, it was nice to see someone actually enforce it. You haven’t been getting it the last four years.” There were signs, earlier in the day, that the debate might be more chaotic. President Trump began his day in a fight with the media, posting an advance video on Facebook of an interview with Lesley Stahl of “60 Minutes,” whom he accused of being “rude” for asking tough questions in the manner of, well, “60 Minutes.” In his Facebook post, he added, “Tonight’s anchor, Kristen Welker, is far worse!” By the middle of the night, though, Mr. Trump — who has not historically taken kindly to being challenged by journalists, especially Black women — was telling her, “By the way, I respect very much the way you’re handling this.” (He still gave fact checkers plenty of material, albeit more at lower volume. “President Trump was better behaved tonight,” said CNN’s Daniel Dale, “but he lied more.”) As for Mr. Biden, like any leader in the polls late in an election, his first goal was to do no harm. But the debate was also a sort of do-over for him, a chance to get off planned arguments that got buried in argle-bargle the first time out. So the challenger played plenty of offense. We got a clear “Malarkey.” He attacked the president on his Covid-19 response — “We’re about to go into a dark winter and he’s got no plan” — while repeatedly displaying a face mask so pointedly you might have thought it was his running mate. He was able to deliver speeches straight to the camera empathizing with families who have lost loved ones to the pandemic. The president countered that his opponent was just speaking like a politician. You could argue about who got the better of these exchanges. But at least you could hear them this time. Mostly, the candidates kept their interjections nonverbal, using the TV split-screen, Mr. Trump scowling and jutting his jaw, Mr. Biden grinning and shaking his head. The debate was least intelligible when Mr. Trump began speaking in Fox News-ese, particularly when raising a baroque, unsubstantiated claim of business corruption against Mr. Biden’s son Hunter, driven mostly by disputed newspaper reports and right-wing talk. I’m not sure what voters took away from his unglossed references to “the big man” and “the laptop” if they didn’t have a Ph.D. in “Hannity.” He did at least take turns speaking, though. But before we go handing out too many gold stars for the president’s “new tone,” it’s worth remembering his likely motives. Mr. Trump, by most accounts, entered this debate in a worse position than he entered the first debate — and most educated guesses are that this was at least in part because of how he behaved in the first debate. The best mute button, it turns out, may be a bad poll number.
