Sunday night’s ruling by U.S. District Judge Amy Totenberg held that it was too late to revamp the voting system. “The capacity of county election systems and poll workers, much less the secretary of state’s office, to turn on a dime and switch to a full-scale hand-marked paper ballot system is contradicted by the entire messy electoral record of the past years,” Judge Totenberg wrote, rejecting arguments by the Coalition for Good Governance that potential security holes in the system compromised the integrity of the election. “Implementation of such a sudden systemic change under these circumstances cannot but cause voter confusion and some real measure of electoral disruption,” she added. Judge Totenberg — who began her ruling by comparing the repetitive wrangling over voting procedures to the movie “Groundhog Day” — did acknowledge there could be some issues with the new system. She directed election officials to beef up efforts to review ballot images to ensure that software does not miss votes before January’s expected runoff for the Senate seat currently held by Kelly Loeffler. The ruling had far-reaching implications in a state with a neck-and-neck presidential race and two hotly contested Senate races. Jill Biden, the wife of the Democratic nominee, Joseph R. Elections officials reported a range of problems when the system was first implemented this year, including inadequate training of poll workers that led to some ballots being placed in scanners upside-down.
