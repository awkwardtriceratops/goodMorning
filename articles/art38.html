Germany will require restaurants and bars to close their doors to patrons starting Monday and professional sports teams to play to empty stadiums, while theaters, gyms and cosmetic studios will be shuttered as the country scrambles to halt a spike in new virus cases. The latest restrictions also limit the number of people allowed to meet up in public, but supermarkets, stores, schools and day care centers will remain open, Chancellor Angela Merkel said on Wednesday. The German media labeled the restrictions, which are less comprehensive than those imposed in the spring, when schools and most businesses were forced to close, as “lockdown light.” The measures aim to ease the strain on the country’s health system, where hospitals have seen the number of patients double in the past 10 days, as new daily cases nudged toward a record 15,000, and halt the rapid spread of the virus before the coming Christmas holidays, without bringing the economy to a complete standstill. The government will compensate small- and mid-sized businesses affected by the monthlong closures with up to 75 percent of losses, the chancellor said. Financial aid for affected business will be worth up to 10 billion euros. “Within weeks we will reach the limits of our health system,” Ms. Merkel said at a news conference, after she reached agreement with Germany’s 16 governors on the nationwide measures, which she conceded were hard, but necessary. “It is completely clear that we must act, and act now to prevent a national health crisis.” Ms. Merkel conceded the restrictions are “burdensome” for a public that has grown increasingly weary of and rebellious against the limitations. For weeks, European countries have tried to slow the spread of the virus through targeted restrictions aimed at avoiding the tough nationwide lockdowns imposed in the spring. But the measures have not succeeded at halting the surge in cases and hospitalizations. A detailed map shows the extent of the coronavirus outbreak, with charts and tables of the number of cases and deaths. France, Spain, Italy and Britain are all among the European countries reporting spikes in new cases — and their highest death tolls in months, according to a Times database. France, where more than two-thirds of the population is already under a 9 p.m. curfew, was also bracing for tightened restrictions on Wednesday ahead of a speech by President Emmanuel Macron. France reported 523 coronavirus deaths on Tuesday, the country’s highest number since April. French business leaders have expressed fears that a new lockdown would be fatal to many companies, but doctors and medical experts said it was necessary. Frédéric Valletoux, president of the French Hospital Federation, said on Wednesday that hospitals were in a much more precarious situation than in the spring, with staff being exhausted from the first wave, less leeway to defer treatments or surgeries to make room for Covid-19 patients, and other epidemics like the flu coming with winter. “Hospitals won’t hold if we don’t take drastic measures,” Mr. Valletoux said. Neighboring Belgium, which hosts the headquarters of the European Union, now has one of the highest per capita rates of new cases in the world. The number of people admitted to hospitals has almost matched the first wave peak of March 28, and experts warn of the risk of overwhelming health care facilities. The Czech Republic, with similar per capita rates to Belgium, imposed a nationwide curfew starting Wednesday. Stores, schools and restaurants were closed, while masks were made mandatory in all public places. Demonstrations against the restrictions were planned for Wednesday in the capital, Prague. European stocks sank to their lowest levels in months on Wednesday amid the echoes of the early days of the pandemic. The Stoxx Europe 600 index and the FTSE 100 index fell more than 2 percent. Wall Street followed suit, with the S&P 500 falling more than 3 percent. In Britain, which reported 367 deaths on Tuesday, Prime Minister Boris Johnson is also facing calls for a new national lockdown, as the government’s advisory body, known as SAGE, warned him that the second wave could be more deadly than the first, according to The Telegraph. With over 61,000 coronavirus deaths, Britain has been the worst-hit country in Europe, with a surge in excess deaths that didn’t reach the peaks seen in Italy or Spain, but which lasted longer. Spain has weighed tougher measures, with the Basque Country closing the region’s borders and the regional authorities in Madrid considering a similar move. In Italy, a curfew for bars and restaurants and the closure of public gyms, cinemas and swimming pools have led to anti-lockdown protests. Portugal on Wednesday made it compulsory for residents to wear a mask outdoors wherever social distancing cannot be guaranteed. The measure, which applies to people from the age of 10, came into force a day after the country registered its second-highest daily rise in cases since the start of the pandemic. Portugal’s government also said it would prohibit people from traveling between municipalities from Oct. 30 until Nov. 3, which will cover a long holiday weekend. The European Union on Wednesday called for improved testing and for better coordination of what has been a patchwork of disparate measures in its member countries. “We are deeply in the second wave now,” Ursula von der Leyen, the president of the European Commission, the bloc’s executive arm, said at a news conference. “We’re dealing with two enemies,” she added. “With the coronavirus itself and corona fatigue,” she said. — Aurelien Breeden, Elian Peltier, Monika Pronczuk, Christopher F. A colleague, overwhelmed by it all, wrote: “Anxiety and depression have been kicking my butt lately.” On assignment for The New York Times, I spent a morning with Jodie Gord, a nurse manager at Aurora St. Luke’s Medical Center, who put the journal in a room she calls the Zen Den, a place of solace just a few doors down from the chaos of the I.C.U. During my visit, the unit was a whirl of urgent energy, with scores of medical workers racing to treat about 20 gravely ill people, some of them on ventilators. Staff members went around the room checking oxygen levels and helping some patients move onto their stomachs, a position that can make breathing easier. As the pandemic has intensified, so has Ms. Gord’s feeling of helplessness as she sees sick patient after sick patient enter the unit. More than 5,300 new cases were identified statewide, on one of the state’s worst days yet. “Mentally, I was really going into a dark, slippery slope,” Ms. Gord told me. “In these last two weeks, I really felt it. The record was broken Tuesday, even as the Trump administration announced what it called its first-term scientific accomplishments, in a news release that included “ENDING THE COVID-19 PANDEMIC,” written in bold, capital letters. The record reflects how quickly the virus is spreading. It took nearly three months for the first 500,000 coronavirus cases to be tallied in the United States — the first was confirmed on Jan. 21, and the country did not reach the half-million mark until April 11. The city joins New York and Wisconsin, states that this month issued restrictions or outright bans on indoor dining in restaurants and bars to limit the spread of the coronavirus. The restrictions have been loudly opposed by a restaurant industry that has been decimated by the pandemic. Chicago is now averaging more than twice as many coronavirus-related hospital admissions per day as it was a month ago, Mr. Pritzker’s office said, and the share of tests that are coming back positive has almost doubled since the beginning of October. The U.S. has reported a record daily average of about 73,000 new cases over the past week, an increase of about 40 percent from the average two weeks earlier. The company will provide 300,000 doses over the next two months, and up to 650,000 doses next year — far less than what is needed to treat patients when there have been an average of more than 73,000 cases per day over the past week, as of Tuesday. Eli Lilly and a competitor, Regeneron, have applied to the F.D.A. for emergency authorization of their antibody treatments, which have both shown early promise in helping people who are newly infected with the coronavirus. Regeneron has received more than $160 million in federal dollars to develop its antibody treatment, and in July signed a $450 million manufacturing deal with the U.S. government to supply up to 300,000 doses of its treatment by the end of the year. Eli Lilly’s chief executive, David A. Ricks, also said on Monday that the treatment, called bamlanivimab, will cost $1,250 in wealthy countries, and that it would charge less in other countries. Mr. Ricks also said it was working with the Bill and Melinda Gates Foundation to supply the therapy to low- and middle-income countries. “What can’t happen, though, is that only the wealthiest countries or people gain access to COVID-19 treatments,” Mr. Ricks said in the statement. “Medicine must be available to patients with the greatest unmet need, not sold to the highest bidder.” Earlier this month, New Jersey’s former governor, Chris Christie, said that he had received special access to Eli Lilly’s treatment after he was hospitalized with Covid-19 — one of a small number of people, the company has said, who received the treatment outside of a clinical trial. President Trump received the Regeneron antibodies shortly after he tested positive for the coronavirus. Under the federal agreements with Eli Lilly and Regeneron, the treatments will be available to Americans free of charge, though health care providers can charge to administer them. The therapies are infused intravenously and must be given in a clinic or hospital. In announcing the price, he acknowledged that Eli Lilly would profit from what it is charging in wealthy countries, saying that the company expects “a modest financial return for our investors by the end of 2021.” Eli Lilly and Regeneron’s treatments are being tested at sites around the country in people newly infected by the virus and in those who have been exposed to it, such as family members of people who tested positive, or nursing home residents where an outbreak has occurred. The treatments are believed to work early in an infection by supplying powerful antibodies to help patients fight the virus. Early data has shown the treatment may reduce patients’ viral loads and prevent visits to the emergency room and hospital. The treatments have not worked so far in sicker patients. Earlier this week, one of Eli Lilly’s trials stopped giving the antibodies to hospitalized patients after they were shown not to work. The treatments will initially be in short supply. If the F.D.A. authorizes them for emergency use, Eli Lilly has said it will have about 100,000 doses immediately available, with up to one million by the end of the year. Regeneron has said it will have about 50,000 right away, and 300,000 by the end of the year. The U.S. is now seeing roughly 73,000 cases a day.
