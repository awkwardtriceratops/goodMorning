United Airlines Reaches Deal to Avoid Job Cuts: Live Business Updates

New Mexico’s privacy lawsuit against Google is dismissed. United Airlines and its pilots’ union agree to save 2,850 jobs as deadline looms. A high valuation for Honest Company will be a test of its turnaround. Wall Street rallies after a rocky stretch. American Airlines chief warns of possible deep job cuts this week. China’s economy is surging as consumers start spending again. United Airlines and its pilots’ union reached an agreement Monday that would avert 2,850 job cuts scheduled to begin as soon as Thursday, when federal restrictions on layoffs under a March stimulus law end. The union’s members agreed to a collective sacrifice to prevent any of United’s 13,000 pilots from being furloughed until June 2021. Under the agreement, some older pilots will also be eligible for buyouts. As part of the CARES Act that Congress passed in the spring, passenger airlines received $25 billion to pay employees on the condition that they avoid any large cuts until Oct. 1. A union-led effort to renew that funding gained bipartisan support this summer, but remains in limbo because negotiations over a broader stimulus measure have stalled. Airline executives joined the effort, lobbying lawmakers in Washington in recent weeks. “We’ve worked for months on creative solutions to mitigate massive pilot layoffs,” Capt. Todd Insler, chairman of the union, the United Master Executive Council, said in a statement. “With this agreement now solidified, we will turn our focus back to Congress to secure a much-needed CARES Act extension to keep our industry solvent until we recover from this pandemic.” United’s pilots have avoided cuts, but the airline is still expected to furlough nearly 12,000 other workers. American Airlines has said that it expects to cut as many as 19,000 employees starting Thursday, while Delta Air Lines has said it may need to furlough nearly 2,000 pilots. Southwest Airlines has said it expects to avoid broad cuts, at least this year. — Niraj Chokshi A federal judge has dismissed a lawsuit filed by New Mexico’s attorney general against Google for violating the online privacy of children in the state, ruling that the company had already received permission from schools to let children use its education software and did not need separate parental approval. In a ruling on Friday, U.S. District Judge Nancy Freudenthal said New Mexico failed to prove that Google had violated the federal Children’s Online Privacy Protection Act, which requires companies to obtain a parent’s consent before collecting personal information on children under 13. Judge Freudenthal said Google had met its requirement because the schools, which had agreed to use the company’s software, served as an agent to the parents. However, she left open the possibility of the state filing an amended complaint against the company. When Hector Balderas, New Mexico’s attorney general, filed the lawsuit in February, he said Google had deceived schools, parents, teachers and students by telling them that there were no privacy concerns with its education tools when the company was collecting information on students’ online activities and locations. “The law is clear that Google must protect our children’s privacy, and we strongly disagree with the Court’s ruling and will continue to litigate to protect child privacy rights,” Mr. Balderas said in a statement. What has been less noticed is that people in countries that had traditionally resisted the e-commerce giant are now also falling into its grasp after retail stores shut down for months because of the coronavirus. The shift has been particularly pronounced in Italy, which was one of the first countries hard hit by the virus. Italians have traditionally preferred to shop in stores and pay cash. But after the government imposed Europe’s first nationwide virus lockdown, Italians began buying items online in record numbers. Even now, as Italy has done better than most places to turn the tide on the virus and people return to stores, the behavioral shift toward e-commerce has not halted. People are using Amazon to buy staples like wine and ham, as well as web cameras, printer cartridges and fitness bands. “The change is real, the change is deep and the change is here to stay,” said David Parma, who has conducted surveys about shifting consumer behavior in Italy for Ipsos in Milan. “Amazon is the biggest winner.” With unemployment about 9 percent nationwide — and closer to 20 percent in areas of southern Italy — many are eager for Amazon to expand.
