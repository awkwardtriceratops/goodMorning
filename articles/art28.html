For Parents, Sending Children Back to School Seemed Too Risky

NEW YORK TODAY By Amanda Rosa [Want to get New York Today by email? Here’s the sign-up.] It’s Wednesday. Weather: Nice and sunny. High around 70. Alternate-side parking: In effect until Nov. 1 (All Saints Day). For many New York City parents, the choice between in-person classes or remote learning wasn’t just about their child’s education. Parents like Margaret Li, of Queens, worried: If she sent her sons back to school, would that put her 68-year-old mother at risk of infection? Since the summer, Mayor Bill de Blasio’s biggest goal was returning New York City’s students to its public schools after the city significantly reduced its coronavirus infection rates. Despite pushback from principals who complained of staff shortages, and despite delays to the start of the school year and concerns about the virus spreading in classrooms, some students went back to in-person classes. But in District 26, a largely immigrant school district in northeastern Queens, the parents of two out of three students stuck with remote learning. “While kids are safe, my mom is safe too,” Ms. Li told my colleague Juliana Kim. “Her health is most important to our family.” [Some worried parents are choosing all-remote instruction for their children.] Half of the district’s population is Asian-American, and seven out of 10 of the city’s Asian-American students opted out of in-person classes. The data is similar for Black and Latino students, as more than half chose all-remote learning after their communities were disproportionately affected by the virus. Several families who spoke to Ms. Kim said they live with older relatives and fear that their children would bring the virus home from school. Parents also said remote learning schedules are more predictable, especially after Gov. Andrew M. Cuomo abruptly closed schools in parts of Brooklyn and Queens where virus positivity rates were high. According to city data, there’s no evidence to suggest that the reopening of schools has contributed to the spread of the virus. Norman write: There’s no place like New York City: Last year, more than 13.5 million international visitors traveled here. (It’s one reason those “I ❤️ NY” T-shirts seemingly fly off the shelves at souvenir shops.) But after the pandemic devastated New York, the tourism industry and the many workers who depend on it have yet to recover, even as plenty of businesses have reopened their doors. “There’s no flights coming in, no tourists visiting,” Jean Metellus, a 71-year-old cabdriver, said as he waited hours for a fare outside Kennedy International Airport. “There’s less people on the streets.” [Data shows that New York City’s tourism industry is reeling.] In August, fewer than 400,000 international passengers arrived at Kennedy, down 89 percent from the more than 3.5 million in the same month last year. The city’s food and beverage sector has lost nearly 200,000 jobs since March. The occupancy rate for hotels is about 40 percent, a decrease from more than 80 percent in August 2019. And the demand for taxis and ride-app services in June was down 71 percent, though lately those numbers have begun to rebound. There is a slight bright side: The road to recovery may begin with locals. A ticket agent for a city tour bus in Times Square said many of his customers were New Yorkers looking for a way to get themselves and their children out of the house. A waitress in Little Italy found it encouraging that many of her diners were from the neighborhood and using the absence of crowds on Mulberry Street to finally try some of the nearby cuisine. I had only been living in the neighborhood for a couple of months, and I was already contemplating whether I should even have come back to the city. Nothing was going quite right, and I didn’t feel at home. Suddenly a torrential downpour started. I ran to take cover under the roof of what looked like a legion club of some sort. An older man stuck his head out the door and said to come in and take a seat. Inside were tables with food and posters of pinup models and sports memorabilia hung on the walls. It was clear that a card game was about to start. A few minutes later, the man who had urged me to come in and a couple of other guys came to me with a huge garbage bag and scissors. They proceeded to make me the nicest trash-bag poncho I had ever seen. We all started laughing as I put it on, and they asked if it was all right. “Perfect,” I said.
